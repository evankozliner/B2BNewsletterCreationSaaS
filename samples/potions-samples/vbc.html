AI's Potential to Revolutionize Primary Care

At our session with VBC Exhibit Hall, we explored the transformative power of AI in primary care with insights from leading experts, Dr. Karthik Ravindran of Kaaya Healthcare and Dr. Jenny Chang from Tufts Medicine Integrated Network.

The conversation illuminated the significant challenges faced by clinicians under the current value-based care model, often likened to navigating a maze without clear pathways. As Dr. Ravindran poignantly stated, many healthcare providers feel overwhelmed by administrative burdens that detract from patient engagement.

“We’re doing things to patients, not for patients,” Dr. Chang emphasized, referencing the siloed nature of patient lists and the inefficiencies that plague daily operations. The overwhelming influx of faxes and paperwork serves as a barrier rather than a facilitator of care.

However, the future is bright. Our discussion highlighted the concept of 'AI-native' primary care, which positions AI not merely as an assistant, but as a proactive partner in managing workflows. Imagine an ecosystem where AI autonomously handles tasks such as scheduling, lab orders, and patient communications, allowing healthcare providers to focus on delivering quality care.

Dr. Ravindran painted a vivid picture of closed-loop care, where every interaction is seamless and every process is tracked to completion. This model fosters trust, a crucial element in healthcare, as Dr. Chang articulated: “Trust is the ultimate cost-saver.”

The future of primary care relies on embracing intelligent solutions that streamline processes and prioritize patient relationships. As we shift towards a more efficient and human-centered model, we can ultimately reclaim what truly matters: trust, time, and attention in patient care.

- Mark

 

GenAI & Value-Based Care: The Partnership That Can Save American Medicine

I've been thinking about a keynote I recently came across by Robert Pearl, M.D. He made a bold but necessary claim that, in the past, healthcare leaders were forced to choose between cost, access, and quality. Today, with generative AI in the picture, that tradeoff is no longer inevitable.

We're spending over $5.5 trillion a year on healthcare in the U.S., more than any other country, and yet we're still falling short on the outcomes that matter. Access is strained. Patients are waiting. Clinicians are burning out. It's clear that the current model isn't working.

But Pearl's talk didn't stop at critique. He pointed toward something more constructive, how AI, particularly generative AI, can act as a true partner to physicians, tool to boost efficiency and fundamentally rewire care delivery. Whether it's triaging patients more intelligently, monitoring them at home, or identifying risk factors earlier, the upside is real, and urgent.

The catch? These capabilities are wasted if we keep tacking them on as fee-for-service layers. If we want to unlock real value here, we most evolve the system itself, toward outcoems instead of volume.

The opportunity is here. But it's on us, as clinicians, operators, and system leaders, to make the leap.

Quote of the Week

What are the bigger and hairier problems right now that you see — both from the practice perspective and the system perspective?

Dr. Karthik: “That’s a hard thing to narrow down. There’s so much that annoys me. But the thing that overarching annoys me is this: Value-based care is sort of this pasture that exists — that city on the hill that Reagan talked about. What we’ve done with all these metrics is create this narrow path we shuttle our practices through to reach that pasture.

The issue is that, in that process, it gets a little claustrophobic. So what ends up happening on a day-to-day basis — what’s overarchingly frustrating — is that when I go see my patients after this talk, my day-to-day feels like I’ve got my face in the butt of another cow and there’s another cow behind me with their butt in my face — or the other way around… and you lose track of it. You see glimpses of it with certain moments or patients, but you lose sight of the greater thing. It’s hard to see the pasture."
